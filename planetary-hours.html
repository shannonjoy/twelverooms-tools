How planetary hours work

Divide the stretch from sunrise to sunset into 12 equal parts, and sunset to the next sunrise into 12 more: that is a planetary hour, and there are 24 of them in a day. They are not 60 minutes each. A daylight hour in June runs longer than an hour on the clock; a daylight hour in December runs shorter. Only near the equinoxes do the two nearly line up.

Each of the 24 belongs to one of the seven classical planets, cycling in a fixed sequence called the Chaldean order: Saturn, Jupiter, Mars, Sun, Venus, Mercury, Moon, then back to Saturn. The first hour after sunrise always belongs to the day's own ruler, which is where the days of the week get their names: Sunday opens with a Sun hour, Monday with a Moon hour, Tuesday Mars, Wednesday Mercury, Thursday Jupiter, Friday Venus, Saturday Saturn.

The traditional use is to match the hour to the matter. Write under Mercury. Ask a favor under Venus or Jupiter. Begin something that needs strength and speed under Mars, sparingly. Common questions

What are planetary hours?

Planetary hours divide daylight into 12 equal parts and night into 12 equal parts, so each hour runs long in summer and short in winter, unlike the fixed 60-minute clock hour. Each of the 24 is ruled by one of the seven classical planets in a fixed rotation, the Chaldean order, starting with the day's own ruler at sunrise.

Why are planetary hours unequal lengths?

They divide the actual daylight and actual night into 12 parts each, so a summer day's 12 hours run longer than 60 minutes apiece and a winter night's do too. Only at the equinoxes do planetary hours match the clock hour closely.
